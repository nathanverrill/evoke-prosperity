EXPLORE (Week 1): Activities 1 – 2
Week 1, Activity 1 (Explore). Follow the Flow
Superpower: Empathetic Changemaker
Primary Evoke Skill: Empathy
Secondary Evoke Skill: Research & Analysis
PFL Domain: Philanthropy
SWBAT: Identify issues in their community that non-profits help solve.
Activity (PBL Description):
Students investigate a pressing and relevant challenge related to water access, property ownership, or financial exclusion. They explore the forces and systems shaping the issue and examine how different members of the community experience its effects. Through observation, questions, and comparison of perspectives, they gather evidence to better understand the challenge before defining it. This understanding will inform the challenge they will address through their Evokation project.
________________


Evoke Mission (direct to students):
Your Mission
If you want to understand this challenge—really understand it—you have to step into it.
Not as yourself.
As them.
And stay curious: ask good questions before jumping to conclusions.
________________


Step 1: Walk in Their World
Choose at least two stakeholders connected to the issue your team is exploring.
They could include:
* someone directly affected by the issue (peer, family member, community member)
* someone trying to help solve the issue (school counselor, nonprofit or community leader)
* someone connected to the larger system (water utility staff, local officials, fire department personnel, or others relevant to your challenge)
Then talk with them.
Listen closely (not just for answers—for emotion, perspective, and patterns)
Ask questions like:
* What does this challenge feel like in your life?
* What worries or frustrates you most about it?
* What matters most to you in this situation?
* Where does money or access to resources play a role?
* How are they currently addressing these issues, if at all?
* Where are they falling short?
Listen for understanding.
Pay attention to what’s not being said.
Ask clarifying questions if something feels incomplete or surprising.
________________


Step 2: Find the Friction
Now compare what you’ve learned as a team:
* Where do their experiences align? 
* Where do they clash? 
This is where the truth hides.
And here’s the hard part:
What’s one assumption you had… that no longer holds up?
Say it out loud.
Write it down.
Own it.
That’s how real understanding begins.
________________


Step 3: Name the Challenge
Now name the challenge.
As a team, create a clear, compelling Challenge Statement that identifies the community issue you want to address through your Evokation (final project) and the financial realities shaping it.
Think about:
* the issue that kept surfacing across the people you spoke with
* how the issue affects individuals or the community
* the systems or barriers making the challenge harder to solve
* where money, access, or resources play a role
* what evidence led you to this conclusion
________________


Evidence:
To complete this mission, your team must submit:
* Notes or reflections from at least two stakeholder perspectives, including one assumption you challenged 
* A Challenge Statement that identifies the community issue and the financial realities shaping it

Week 3, Activity 5 (Imagine). What Would It Take—for Real?
Superpower: Systems Thinker
Primary Evoke Skill: Research & Analysis
Secondary Evoke Skill: Problem Solving
PFL Domain: Budgeting
SWBAT: Students will list sources of income, describe expenditures, and categorize spending into essential and non-essential purchases 
Activity (PBL Description):
Teams begin turning their vision into something more concrete by researching and analyzing what is required to get started. They look at key resources and likely costs, identify what is essential versus what can wait, and create a light starter budget for their own innovative solution. Teams build their budget over two missions. In this first mission, they apply budgeting concepts through a simple project budget that will be refined in the next mission.
________________


Your Mission
You’ve imagined your solution.
You’ve pictured the future it could help create.
Now ask the practical question:
What would it actually take to get started?
________________


Step 1: Identify What You Need
Every idea—no matter how powerful—needs resources.
Break it down:
   * People – Who needs to be involved? 
   * Time – How long might it take to get started? 
   * Partnerships – Who could support or unlock progress? 
   * Space & Tools – What physical or digital resources might be needed? 
   * Money – What costs might need to be covered? 
Don’t just guess.
Use evidence to test your assumptions.
Research:
   * simple project budget template 
   * startup or community project checklists 
   * real prices for people, tools, materials, or services your idea might need 
   * look up publicly available 990s to understand how existing organizations budget.
You’re gathering and analyzing information to make smarter estimates and build your starter budget.

________________

So, sort what you identified:

This is budgeting.
It’s deciding what’s essential to get started.

Step 3: Build a Starter Budget
Find an appropriate project budget template online to estimate the costs of getting your idea started.

This is your team’s first draft—not the final version.

Step 1: The Venture Points Challenge
Every venture needs support.
Your team has 100 Venture Points representing the total value of the venture.
The amount of points each person has corresponds to their power in the venture. For example, if you have 60 venture points and are voting on a decision, you get 60 votes. 
Keeping your points means you maintain control of your venture, but do all the work yourselves.
Offering your points means you bring in additional resources (time, money, expertise) to support your venture, but you share the control.
Now decide:
How many Venture Points are you willing to offer to attract support?
This is not exact finance.
It is strategic thinking.
For example:
Keep 90 / Offer 10 → high confidence, low outside dependence: you maintain control over decisions, but lose outside support.
Keep 60 / Offer 40 → moderate dependence, shared support: you maintain majority control over decisions, but increase outside support to grow venture.
Keep 30 / Offer 70 → higher need, bigger outside investment: you offer up the majority control because you believe so strongly in your vision, but need significant support to execute your plans.
Your choice should match your venture’s risk profile.
A LOW RISK venture may offer fewer points because the risk is lower and the path is more predictable.
A MEDIUM RISK venture may offer a moderate share because the opportunity is meaningful and support still matters.
A HIGH RISK venture may offer more because the need is higher—but so is the potential upside.
Think about:
   * how much outside help you need to get your opportunity off the ground
   * what kind of return your backers might expect
   * whether your choice matches your team’s goals and risk tolerance profile
Complete this sentence:
We are offering ____ Venture Points because ____________________.
This becomes part of your pitch.
________________


Step 2: Build Your Pitch
Now craft your message.
Your job is to make someone believe your solution is worth backing.
That means communicating clearly—not just talking.
Your pitch should clearly answer:
• What problem are you solving?
• What is your solution and how does your prototype bring it to life?
• Why is it worth backing?
• How much will it cost to develop and launch?
• What do those costs consist of?
• What impact or return could it create?
• What support are you asking for?
Keep it concise.
Make it memorable.
________________


Step 3: Design the Experience
Communication is more than words.
It is also about visuals, delivery, teamwork, and what your audience takes away.
How will your team deliver the pitch?
Decide:
   * who will speak
   * what each team member will do
   * what you will show 
   * how you want your audience to remember your idea
Your pitch might include:
   * your prototype
   * props
   * visuals
   * a slide deck

Activity (PBL Description)
Teams publicly present their Evoke journey, bringing together the challenge they identified, the innovation they designed, the prototype they created, and the strategic choices they made about success, risk, and support. As part of the experience, teams build a trusting relationship with their audience by communicating clearly, adapting in the moment, and responding thoughtfully to questions. In doing so, they practice respectful engagement with different perspectives and strengthen relationships through how they listen and respond as a team and connect with others in real time.
