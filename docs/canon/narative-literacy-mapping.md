2. Ending Revision
   The ending becomes much more believable if the mountain still has problems.
   Instead of
   Everyone has clean water.
   Everyone is happy.
   The ending should be
   The storms still come.
   Winters are still hard.
   Markets still change.
   But the community can adapt.
   A good ending isn't
   "Problems disappeared."
   It's
   "People became capable of solving the next problem."
   That's a much stronger systems-thinking message.
   Instead of:
   Prosperity for everyone.
   Think:
   Opportunity for anyone willing to build.

The difference is that failure is no longer catastrophic because the institutions are resilient thanks to Alex’s contributions. Instead of a one stop solution to everything, the ending will be adjusted to reflect that problems will still come. However, thanks to Alex’s industrial advancements and assistance to those in each city, the people are given assets in order to build and solve their own solutions for future problems.

3. The Oasis
   Right now it almost reads like:
   Oasis = perfect, Halyard - mid, Keel = bad
   Instead:
   Oasis
   Advanced, Efficient, Competitive, Innovative.
   But, with this also comes the issues of...
   expensive
   highly regulated
   lots of opportunity
   lots of pressure
   People work hard there and companies compete. Some ideas fail, but some succeed.
   It's simply a mature economy. Alex shouldn't want to live in the Oasis forever. He wants to learn from it the same way he learned other lessons from Halyard and Keel.

Think of it as:
"The Oasis solved some problems, but it also created new ones."

Likewise Halyard becomes
where people build.
Keel becomes
where people belong.
Neither is objectively "better."
They simply have different strengths.

The overall theme changes
Instead of
"We climbed to paradise."
It becomes
"We climbed to learn."
Alex doesn't rescue the mountain, but instead he brings back knowledge. With each step: Keel, Halyard, Oasis, Alex gains knowledge through different stressors and lessons. With this, he passes on the information he learns back to the people. The mountain rescues itself.

B1llbot's final lesson
The last thing B1llbot says should encapsulate Bill Reynolds' worldview:
"Every generation inherits a system.
They don't get to choose that.
They do get to choose whether they leave it stronger than they found it."
Or perhaps even simpler:
"The goal was never a perfect mountain.
The goal was a mountain that could stand on its own."

A Larger Narrative
Taken together, the five infographics evolve into more than a story about a mountain. They become a progression of financial and civic education:
Infographic 1: Stewardship — Budget scarce resources and earn value.
Infographic 2: Resilience — Diversify, manage risk, and understand systems.
Infographic 3: Incentives — Recognize how markets, monopolies, and ownership shape outcomes.
Infographic 4: Wealth Creation — Invest in assets, community infrastructure, and institutions that compound over time.
Infographic 5: Leadership — Scale proven systems, mentor others, and create conditions where communities can thrive independently.
